Thread: "Real talk about CAPS and mental health resources at UNC" (r/UNC, 987 upvotes)

Top comment:
CAPS (Counseling and Psychological Services) is located in the James A. Taylor Building on South Road. Here is what you need to know that the university doesn't tell you clearly:

WHAT CAPS ACTUALLY OFFERS:
- Initial consultation: Free, same-day or next-day for urgent needs. This is called an "initial consultation" not an appointment. You can walk in during business hours.
- Short-term individual therapy: CAPS does short-term (typically 6-10 sessions) individual counseling. This is free.
- Ongoing therapy: If you need more than short-term, they refer you to community providers. They have a list of therapists who work on sliding scale fees for students.
- Crisis services: CAPS has same-day crisis appointments. If you're in crisis, say so when you call - you will be seen same day.
- Groups: CAPS runs several free therapy groups (anxiety, depression, identity-based groups). These are actually very good and have less of a waitlist than individual therapy.

THE WAITLIST REALITY:
Demand for CAPS significantly exceeds capacity, especially in October and February (common high-stress periods). Initial consultations are fast; ongoing individual therapy has a waitlist of weeks. If you think you might need therapy, start the process EARLY in the semester before you're in crisis.

Reply (654 upvotes):
If CAPS is backed up, other free/low-cost options:
- UNC Employee Assistance Program (EAP): technically for employees but some grad students have accessed this
- Psychology department's training clinic offers therapy at very low cost (students practice under supervision)
- Many community health centers in Orange County offer sliding-scale fees
- Betterhelp and similar apps have financial aid programs - ask about them
- The After Hours Line (1-877-626-3477) is available 24/7 for mental health crises if CAPS is closed

Reply (445 upvotes):
Disability accommodations through ARS (Accessibility Resources & Services): If you have a diagnosed mental health condition (depression, anxiety, ADHD, etc.) that affects your academic performance, you can register with ARS for accommodations. Common accommodations include extended test time, reduced-distraction testing, flexible attendance, and extended deadlines. You need documentation from a provider. The process takes a few weeks so don't wait until you're struggling to start it.

Thread: "Navigating burnout at UNC - how do you actually recover?" (r/UNC)

Top comment (523 upvotes):
I burned out hard during my sophomore year and here's what actually helped vs. what everyone tells you but doesn't work:

WHAT ACTUALLY HELPS:
- Dropping a course if you're overwhelmed. A W on your transcript is recoverable. A semester of failing grades is not. Talk to your advisor.
- Taking a medical withdrawal if things are really bad. UNC's withdrawal policy is more flexible than people think for documented medical situations.
- Talking to your professors early. Most professors have more flexibility than they advertise if you come to them BEFORE the deadline, not after.
- Actually taking breaks - the guilty-rest cycle (taking a break but feeling bad about it the whole time) provides zero recovery.
- Identifying what specifically is burning you out. "Everything" is not actionable. Pinpoint one thing.

WHAT PEOPLE TELL YOU BUT DOESN'T HELP:
- "Just time block better" - if you're burned out, no productivity system fixes the underlying issue
- "Go to the gym" - exercise helps maintenance but doesn't fix a structural overcommitment problem
- "Talk to someone" without any guidance on who and how to access them (hence this post)

Thread: "Practical money tips for UNC students" (r/UNC)

Top comment (412 upvotes):
Financial survival guide that nobody gives you:

- The Dean of Students Office has an emergency loan fund (not a grant - you repay it) for situations like unexpected medical bills, car repair, or temporary food insecurity. You don't have to be in extreme poverty to qualify. Apply through their website.
- Carolina Away Card / University discounts: Your OneCard gets you discounts at many local businesses. Check the UNC student discount database.
- Textbooks: Use the library's course reserves before buying anything. Many required textbooks are available at Davis Library for 2-hour in-library loans (enough to do assigned readings). Also check OpenLibrary.org and Anna's Archive before buying.
- The Student U emergency fund exists specifically for first-generation and lower-income students.
- Health insurance: If you're on your parents' plan, verify it covers care in NC. If not, the UNC Student Health Insurance Plan is available and often partially subsidizable through financial aid.

Thread: "Honest guide to the UNC CS department from a senior" (r/UNC)

Top comment (892 upvotes):
I'm graduating with a CS degree and wanted to write the guide I wish existed. The department has improved a lot in recent years but there are still things they won't tell you at orientation.

COURSES YOU NEED TO KNOW ABOUT:

COMP 401/410 - The Intro Sequence:
401 is the real weed-out course. 410 (Data Structures) is harder but by then people who can't handle it have switched majors. 401 with KMP is notoriously difficult. Some students take it at a community college over summer if they want a lighter version, though the UNC version is more thorough. Don't do this your first semester unless you have prior Java experience.

COMP 283 (Discrete Math):
Many CS majors dread this course but it's actually very manageable if you keep up with the problem sets. The prof matters enormously here - check Rate My Professors before registering. The concepts (logic, sets, proofs, graph theory) come up constantly in later CS courses. Do not blow this off.

COMP 311 (Systems):
The course that separates people who actually understand computers from people who just code. Covers C, assembly, memory, processes. Hard. Very worth it. The professor team has been more consistent in recent years. Lab sections are mandatory and count toward your grade. Start labs early - they take longer than you think.

COMP 455 (Models of Languages and Computation):
Theory course. Automata, Turing machines, complexity. Surprisingly approachable with the right mindset. Many people find it clicks much better than expected. The professor has been known to give hints about exam format in the last lecture before exams - show up.

COMP 523 (Software Engineering Lab):
The capstone for many. You work with a real client on a real project over a full semester. Group dynamics can make or break this. Communication with your client matters as much as technical skill. Past clients have included nonprofits and university departments. The presentations at the end are public.

INTERNSHIP / CAREER:
Reply (567 upvotes):
Career tips specific to UNC CS:

- The CS department career fair is separate from the general UNC career fair. Check department emails for the date - it's usually in September and February.
- Professors doing research actively recruit undergrads. Email them directly and attach your resume. "Do you have research opportunities for undergrads?" is a perfectly fine email to send. Most will respond.
- The CS department has a Slack and Discord. Get on both. Job leads and interview prep resources are shared there.
- UNC CS alumni network is more active than you'd expect. LinkedIn outreach to UNC CS alumni gets a surprisingly high response rate - mention the school specifically.

Reply (423 upvotes):
Advising tip: The CS academic advisors are helpful but overloaded. Book your advising appointment the day they open for the semester - they fill up in hours. If you have a simple question (does this course count for X requirement?) email before booking an appointment to save both your time. For course substitutions and petitions, you need to fill out a form and get a faculty signature - start that process early because professors are slow to sign things near the end of semester.

Thread: "Which COMP electives are actually worth taking?" (r/UNC)

Top comment (334 upvotes):
Here's the unofficial ranking of CS electives based on usefulness and quality:

Worth it (resume and learning value):
- COMP 426 Modern Web Programming: directly applicable to jobs, good professor, real projects
- COMP 421 (Files & Databases): useful for almost every software job, teaches SQL and database design
- COMP 433 (Mobile App Development): you build actual apps, portfolio-worthy
- COMP 520 (Compilers): hard but respected, shows depth

Skip or approach carefully:
- Any elective where the sole assignment is a research paper and no code. For CS majors these tend to feel disconnected.
- Check Rate My Professors before any elective - instructor quality varies enormously.

Niche but underrated:
- COMP 431 (Internet Services): covers how the internet actually works (DNS, HTTP, TCP/IP). Great if you want backend or devops roles.
- COMP 411 (Computer Organization): goes deeper on hardware than 311. Good if you're interested in systems or embedded.
